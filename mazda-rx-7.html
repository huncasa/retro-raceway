Mazda RX-7 FD

A Technical Masterpiece

The Mazda RX-7 FD is a sports car produced by Japanese automaker Mazda from 1991 to 2002. It was the third generation of the RX-7 series and was designed with the philosophy of "Jinba Ittai", which translates to "horse and rider as one". This design philosophy was aimed at providing a car that would feel like an extension of the driver's body, allowing for an unparalleled driving experience. The RX-7 FD was powered by a twin-turbocharged 13B rotary engine, which produced 255 horsepower and 217 lb-ft of torque in its stock form. However, the engine was known for being highly tunable, and many RX-7 owners would modify their cars to produce well over 400 horsepower. This made the RX-7 FD a favorite among car enthusiasts and tuners, as it was an affordable sports car that could be made into a true performance machine. In terms of its racing history, the RX-7 FD had a successful career in both professional and amateur racing. In Japan, the RX-7 FD was a popular choice for time attack events, where it would go head-to-head with other high-performance Japanese sports cars. In the United States, the RX-7 FD was a dominant force in the IMSA GTU racing series, where it won several championships in the early 1990s. One of the most notable achievements of the RX-7 FD in racing was its victory at the 1991 24 Hours of Le Mans. The car was entered in the GT class by Mazdaspeed, the motorsports division of Mazda, and was driven by Johnny Herbert, Volker Weidler, and Bertrand Gachot. The RX-7 FD completed 362 laps and covered a distance of 4,612.8 km, securing the first and only victory for a Japanese manufacturer at the iconic endurance race.

A Collector's Dream Car

The RX-7 FD's success in racing can be attributed to its lightweight chassis, excellent handling, and powerful engine. Its rotary engine had a high power-to-weight ratio, which made it an ideal choice for endurance racing. In addition, the car's suspension system was designed with racing in mind, with double wishbones at the front and a multi-link setup at the rear. Despite its success in racing, the RX-7 FD was eventually phased out due to changing emissions regulations in Japan. However, its legacy lives on, and it remains a beloved sports car among car enthusiasts and tuners alike. Its sleek design, powerful rotary engine, and impressive racing pedigree have cemented its place in automotive history as one of the greatest sports cars ever made.
