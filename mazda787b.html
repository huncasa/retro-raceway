Mazda 787B

A Revolutionary Race Car

The Mazda 787B is an iconic race car that has left an indelible mark on the history of motorsport. This car was the first (and to date, only) Japanese car to win the prestigious 24 Hours of Le Mans race, a feat that has solidified its place in automotive history. In this article, we will take a deep dive into the Mazda 787B, exploring its design, engineering, and racing accomplishments. The Mazda 787B was developed in the late 1980s as part of Mazda's Group C racing program. Group C was a set of regulations created by the FIA for sports car racing, which allowed manufacturers to build advanced, purpose-built race cars that could reach incredible speeds and cornering abilities. The 787B was designed to take advantage of these regulations and push the boundaries of what was possible in motorsport. At the heart of the Mazda 787B is a four-rotor Wankel engine that produces 700 horsepower at 9,000 rpm. This engine is a unique design that eschews traditional pistons and cylinders for a more compact, lightweight rotary design. The engine is mounted in the middle of the car, behind the driver, and powers the rear wheels through a five-speed manual transmission. With this setup, the 787B was capable of reaching a top speed of over 200 mph. One of the most impressive aspects of the Mazda 787B was its acceleration. Thanks to its lightweight construction and powerful engine, the 787B could accelerate from 0 to 60 mph in just 2.9 seconds, and reach 100 mph in just 6.1 seconds. This acceleration allowed the car to quickly pull away from its competitors on the straights and maintain a lead throughout the race.

Triumph at Le Mans

The Mazda 787B was not just fast, but also well-engineered. The car featured a carbon fiber monocoque chassis, which provided a strong and lightweight foundation for the car's components. The suspension system was a double-wishbone setup with adjustable coil-over dampers, allowing the driver to fine-tune the car's handling for different track conditions. The brakes were carbon-ceramic discs with four-piston calipers, providing immense stopping power to match the car's incredible speed. The Mazda 787B made its racing debut in 1990 at the 24 Hours of Daytona, where it finished in 7th place overall. The car's first major success came later that year at the 24 Hours of Le Mans, where the 787B took the checkered flag after completing 362 laps of the grueling circuit. The car was driven by a team of three drivers: Johnny Herbert, Bertrand Gachot, and Volker Weidler. This victory marked the first time a Japanese car had won the 24 Hours of Le Mans, and cemented the Mazda 787B's place in automotive history. In conclusion, the Mazda 787B is a remarkable machine that pushed the boundaries of what was possible in motorsport. With its lightweight design, powerful engine, and precise engineering, the 787B was able to win one of the most prestigious races in the world and solidify its place in the annals of automotive history.
